Selected Use Cases

Building a Deep‑Tech Venture from Academic R&D to Commercial Hardware

A research team had developed promising results in advanced additive manufacturing but lacked a structured path to transform the technology into a scalable product and viable company. They needed rapid hardware development, early market validation, and a clear venture architecture to determine commercial potential.

  • Advanced and digital manufacturing architecture for high‑precision, tool‑less production
  • Lean startup and stage‑gate innovation development
  • RCA methodologies (Ishikawa, 5 Whys, PFMEA) for process robustness
  • Digital manufacturing concepts including process windows and parameter interplay
  • Industry 4.0 readiness and early digital‑twin considerations

Outcome: Transition from academic prototype to first commercial hardware prototype in six months, with stable processing windows enabling reliable high‑precision production at scale.

Product and Manufacturing Innovation for Next‑Generation Optical Components

A manufacturer of optical components faced increasing competitive pressure as small, high‑precision parts had become economically unviable to produce in Europe. To regain competitiveness and access emerging markets such as AR/VR wearables and automotive HUD systems, they required a new production concept and a clear technology and product strategy.

  • Product and market innovation for emerging optical applications
  • New Product Introduction (NPI) and cross‑industry translation of manufacturing equipment
  • Material innovation enabling novel processing approaches
  • Semi‑automated mass‑production concepts for micro‑optics
  • Technology roadmap and capability build‑up

Outcome: Product and manufacturing innovation enabling competitive mass production of small, high‑precision optical components in Europe, opening new business opportunities in future markets.

Automated Assembly Line for High‑Precision Optical Components

A producer of optical components needed to reduce manual assembly effort, improve yield, and increase throughput. Multiple process steps — cleaning, gluing, wet‑coating — were performed manually, resulting in variability, high labor intensity, and limited scalability.

  • Hands‑on engineering execution and supplier qualification (Europe/Asia)
  • Automation architecture and multi‑step process integration
  • MES–ERP integration for traceability and operational transparency
  • Operations engineering with focus on OEE, WIP, and cycle‑time optimization
  • Training and knowledge transfer for new production capabilities

Outcome: Automated precision‑assembly line reducing manual handling by 90% and lowering total production cost at scale by over 70%, while enabling new applications and markets.

Technology and Innovation Assessment for Industrial M&A Targets

An investment team required a structured, technology‑driven assessment of potential M&A targets across several industrial sectors. They needed a consistent framework to compare companies with different product portfolios, technology stacks, and market positions, and to understand their innovation potential and strategic fit.

  • Technology evaluation and innovation roadmap assessment
  • Strategic fit analysis using structured frameworks and risk matrices
  • Cross‑industry comparison of product, technology, and IP portfolios
  • Market and ecosystem analysis using Porter’s Five Forces
  • Structured decision support for investment committees

Outcome: Analytical, cross‑industry comparison of industrial M&A targets with structured scorecards clarifying technological attractiveness, innovation potential, and investment risks.
