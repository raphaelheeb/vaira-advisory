Deep‑Tech Venture Architecture

Building a Scalable Deep‑Tech Venture from Academic R&D

A research team had developed a promising additive‑manufacturing process but lacked a structured path from academic experimentation to a scalable hardware product. They needed clarity on technical feasibility, early market validation, and a venture architecture capable of turning laboratory insights into a manufacturable, commercially relevant technology.

  • Digital manufacturing architecture — structured process windows and parameter interplay for high‑precision, tool‑less production
  • Lean hardware development — stage‑gate and rapid prototyping to validate technology and product assumptions early
  • Root‑cause analysis frameworks — Ishikawa, 5 Whys, and PFMEA to stabilize early‑stage processes
  • Process and data modeling — mapping critical variables for predictable material behavior and repeatability
  • Industry 4.0 readiness — early digital‑twin and data‑capture concepts for scalable production

Outcome: A stable processing window and structured development approach enabled the transition from academic experimentation to a manufacturable hardware prototype within six months, providing a clear path toward scalable production and early commercial validation.

Product & Manufacturing Strategy

Defining a Competitive Product and Manufacturing Strategy for Optical Components

A European optics manufacturer faced rising cost pressure and shrinking competitiveness in small, high‑precision components. To access emerging markets such as AR/VR and automotive displays, they required a new production concept, a differentiated product strategy, and a clear understanding of which technologies and materials could unlock viable mass‑production in Europe.

  • Product and market innovation — identifying viable applications in AR/VR, automotive optics, and emerging micro‑optics markets
  • NPI and equipment translation — adapting cross‑industry manufacturing technologies for optical components
  • Material innovation — enabling new processing approaches through functional material development
  • Semi‑automated production concepts — scalable architectures for micro‑optics manufacturing in Europe
  • Technology roadmap definition — structured capability build‑up aligned with long‑term product strategy

Outcome: A differentiated product and manufacturing strategy unlocked competitive European production of micro‑optical components and positioned the company to enter emerging markets such as AR/VR and automotive optical systems.

Precision Engineering Execution

Engineering an Automated Precision Assembly Line for Optical Components

An optical‑components producer relied on manual assembly steps that limited throughput, created variability, and constrained scalability. To meet growing demand and reduce cost per part, they needed an integrated automation concept, qualified suppliers, and a robust engineering execution approach that could unify multiple precision processes into a stable, high‑yield production line.

  • Automation architecture — integrating cleaning, gluing, coating, and inspection into a unified automated line
  • Supplier qualification — hands‑on evaluation and alignment with partners across Europe and Asia
  • MES–ERP integration — traceability, data capture, and operational transparency across all process steps
  • Operations engineering — OEE, WIP, and cycle‑time optimization for stable high‑precision throughput
  • Capability transfer — training and documentation to embed new production knowledge in the client team

Outcome: A fully integrated automation concept reduced manual handling by 90%, improved yield stability, and lowered cost per part by more than 70%, enabling predictable high‑precision throughput at scale.

Technology & Innovation Assessment

Evaluating Technology and Innovation Potential for Industrial M&A Targets

An investment team evaluating industrial M&A targets required a consistent, technology‑driven framework to compare companies with different product portfolios, maturity levels, and innovation trajectories. They needed a structured assessment of technological attractiveness, competitive positioning, and long‑term scalability to support confident, evidence‑based investment decisions.

  • Technology evaluation — assessing maturity, scalability, and differentiation of industrial technologies
  • Innovation roadmap analysis — identifying gaps, risks, and acceleration opportunities
  • Cross‑industry comparison — benchmarking product, technology, and IP portfolios across sectors
  • Market and ecosystem analysis — structured assessment using Porter’s Five Forces and adjacent‑market mapping
  • Decision support — clear scorecards for investment committees to compare targets consistently

Outcome: A structured, technology‑driven assessment produced clear scorecards comparing industrial M&A targets, enabling confident investment decisions based on technological maturity, innovation potential, and long‑term scalability.
